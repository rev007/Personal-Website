Football Squares the Game

An App Story

Football Squares the Game was designed for the iPad and allows users to play football squares interactively. Instead of getting stuck with lousy squares after the initial drawing, players can take advantage of its main feature to prolong the excitement beyond what a conventional game can do.

Let’s go through the user experience (ux) design process and explain the overall goals for improving the product and delivering a better experience.

Research

Okay, so we’ve all played football squares, and we know what they are, right? If not, your job is to do a little research by getting a quick primer at the It’s Hip to Play Squares blog. Otherwise, you may find it difficult to follow along.

Our research consists of defining the problem, performing a competitive analysis, and completing a discovery phase with potential users. We want to figure out who might use our app and how they go about playing football squares right now.

If you read the blog, Lindsay's write-up for Super Bowl (aka football) squares captures the spirit of the game nicely. Playing football squares at a Super Bowl party can undoubtedly ignite interest in a sporting event that historically has been disappointing at times and often lopsided. But there is one major problem - what if you don't get any decent squares?

The number one complaint heard by naturally observing the participants is that if you don't get good squares at the onset, then there is little chance of winning. Once the squares are randomly selected, the game is all but over for some players and whatever interest was generated at the beginning immediately starts to fade.

The problem is that there are only three numbers (zero, three and seven) that account for almost half of all the winning possibilities. This isn't to say that all the other spots on the board are terrible (although there are some). Almost every position has a slim chance of winning on its own, but when you control more squares they add up, and your odds increase.

Let's use the board game Monopoly to illustrate this. For each property you own, the chances that an opponent will have to pay you rent increase by roughly two to three percent. Football squares, on the other hand, ranges from zero to about four percent. In fact, the reason why the big three numbers dominate is that there are so many squares that have almost no chance of winning.

You can see how weighted and overwhelming the odds are in Figure 1. The nine squares represented by the big three numbers account for a 47% chance of winning. On the other hand, it takes the remaining 91 squares to reach 53%.

In a nutshell, we now know the problem. We also know it's not just a preference, and our design will have to ultimately solve it. The bigger challenge is making the solution easy enough for users to take advantage of.

On the surface, football squares seems simple and mundane but it in reality it can get complex and potentially spiral out of control if we're not careful about how we implement our solution. The last thing we want is for people to feel bewildered or yearning to bust out a calculator. Otherwise, the experience will be ruined.

We'll take a closer look at the solution later and decide how to measure whether it was successful or not. We'll also make sure our solution is thoughtful and can be communicated easily, which is just as important.
